ONCE UPON A TIME – long, long ago – there was a queen who in winter was sitting by the open window embroidering. 'Ouch!' she cried suddenly, for

she had pricked her finger. A drop of blood fell on the snow under the window. 'I wish, I wish,' said the queen, 'that I had a daughter with a skin as white as this snow, with hair as black as the ebony of this window-frame, and with lips as red as this

drop of blood.' And what do you think? After a while a daughter was

born, and the girl did indeed have hair as black as ebony, a skin as white as snow, and lips as red as blood. So the king and queen were quite happy, you understand,

and they called their daughter Snow White.


But one day the queen became ill, even so badly that she died. The king, who did not like it, so alone, after a while married again.

His new wife was very beautiful, but what the king did not know was that she thought of nothing else all day long but being beautiful. In her room she even had a magic mirror for that purpose.


Every morning she would stand in front of this mirror and ask:


Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?


And because it was a magic mirror, he could talk, and he always answered:


You are the most beautiful in the land .



The queen then sat down contentedly on a chair in front of her dressing table to make herself even more beautiful.

But one day things went differently. The queen stood in front of the mirror again and asked:


Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?

And do you know what she heard now?


Oh, Queen, you are the most beautiful in this room,

but Snow White is a thousand times more beautiful than you.


The queen could not believe her ears. She pushed aside the curtain and saw Snow White playing, and knew at once that the mirror was right. 'No one must be more beautiful than I,' the queen growled. And then she thought of something very evil.


She called the palace hunter to her and gave him a large knife. 'Tomorrow you will take Snow White into the forest,' she said, 'and there you will stab her to death with this knife.' Then she gave him a small box.

"And to make sure she's dead, I want this box back with Snow White's heart in it."


The next day the huntsman went with Snow White into the forest. But when he took out his knife, he felt sorry for her. 'Go away, Snow White,' he said, 'as far away as possible, because the queen wants me to kill you.' Frightened, Snow White ran as fast as she could, deep into the forest. And do you know what the huntsman did? He killed a wild boar and put its heart in the queen's box.


Meanwhile, Snow White kept running. She ran over seven mountains until, exhausted, she came to a tiny house. The front door was not locked.

Snow White had to duck when she went inside.

There must be seven children living here, she thought. Look, there are seven little chairs, seven little plates, seven little glasses and seven little spoons. She

walked up a small narrow staircase and at the top she saw seven little beds in a row. Suddenly she felt how tired she was. (Yes, what do you expect, when you have run so hard over seven mountains!) And before she knew it she was stretched out across three beds and fell into a deep sleep.


Because of that she didn't hear the residents coming home. They weren't seven

children but seven dwarfs, and they saw right away that someone had been in the house. They crept upstairs and discovered the sleeping Snow White.

"Oh, how sweet she is," said one dwarf. "And how beautiful," said another.

"Shhh, don't wake them!" said a third. And they quietly crept back down the stairs.


After a long time Snow White woke up and when she found the seven dwarfs downstairs, she told them what had happened to her. The dwarfs had heard of the evil queen. 'They say she can do magic,' they whispered and crept closer together.


The next morning the dwarves had to go to the silver mine to work as usual. At the door they said to Snow White: 'Be careful, don't let anyone in. You never know!' They waved to her and disappeared behind the mountains.



Meanwhile, in the palace, the hunter had given the box to the evil queen.


"Now I'm the most beautiful again," she said with satisfaction and quickly went to stand in front of the magic mirror.


Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?


The mirror replied:


Oh, Queen, you are the most beautiful in this room,

but Snow White is a thousand times more beautiful than you.


The queen showed the box to the mirror. 'Ha! That can't be!

Snow White is dead. Here is her heart!' But the mirror answered:


Oh, Queen, the fairest in this room are you, but Snow White beyond the Seven Mountains in the house of the

Seven Dwarfs is a thousand times fairer than you!


The queen understood that the huntsman had fooled her. She trembled with rage.

“Then I'll kill her myself,” she said, gritting her teeth.


She went down six flights of stairs to the cellar and there conjured up a poisoned apple. It was red on one side and green on the other. She changed herself into an old woman and set off, over the seven mountains, to the house of the seven dwarves.

"I'll get her!" she growled again and again. And in her basket, among the other apples, lay the poisoned apple... Snow White, who was left alone in

the cottage of the seven dwarfs, was just about to put a pan of soup on the fire, when there was a tap at the window. It was an old woman, who was carrying a basket of apples.


'May I rest here for a while? I am so tired,' she panted. Snow White hesitated. She opened the window and said, 'I am sorry, old woman, the dwarves will not let anyone in. But I can give you a glass of water.' When the witch had

finished the glass, she said, 'Since you have been so kind to me, I will give you an apple. Look, a very beautiful red-green apple. Take a nice

bite!' Snow White took a bite and… fell dead on the ground.


'Hahahaha,' laughed the wicked queen (for that is what it was of course!), 'hahahaha! Now I am the fairest of them all again!' And she hurried back to her

palace, on the other side of the seven mountains.

When the dwarfs came home they found Snow White lying dead on the floor. The dwarfs were deeply and deeply sad, so sad that they could not say goodbye to her. They made a beautiful glass coffin for her and put her in it, so that they could always look at her.


A few days later a prince happened to be riding through the forest on his horse and saw the glass coffin. He thought Snow White was so beautiful that he lifted the glass lid and gave her a kiss.


Apparently Snow White was not completely dead, because she started coughing in fright and the poisoned piece of apple shot out of her throat.

She sat up in surprise and asked, "Where am I? What happened?"

“Hurray!” shouted the dwarves.

How happy they were that Snow White was alive again! The prince was too. He took Snow White on his horse and they rode off together to his palace.

There they got married and lived happily ever after.


And the evil queen? She heard from the mirror that Snow White was still the most beautiful. She found that so terrible that she dropped dead.

Yes, really, she fell over in sheer rage, just like that, right there in front of the mirror.

But that's how it goes in fairy tales.

The next day the father said, 'We're all going to the forest.' And off they went, one after the other, in a long line. The father in front and Tom Thumb last of all. Every five steps he secretly dropped a pebble.

Go and play here, and I will pick you up later.' When the sun had set

The father and mother were very happy to see them, because the father had been able to sell some wood that day and so there was bread in the house again.


But after a while it went wrong again. Nobody bought wood from the woodcutter and there they all sat at the table again in front of empty plates.

So the father brought the children the next day for the second time

to the forest, and again Tom Thumb had his pockets full of pebbles which

he dropped one by one on the way. And again he could find his way home in the evening. Coincidentally, the father had also been able to earn something that day, and so everyone was happy again.

There they stood! How were they going to get home?

But they got lost and went deeper and deeper into the forest, further and further from home.

The brothers grew tired and began to grumble that it was all Tom Thumb's fault, but Tom Thumb did not lose heart. He climbed a tall tree and hoped that he could see their house from there. That was not the case, but he did see a light burning in the distance.

Maybe they could get something to eat there, because they were so hungry!

out of bed and crept into the room of the giant's daughters. They were sleeping

The Emperor's New Clothes

A LONG TIME AGO there was an emperor who had an incredible vain. Now everyone is a little vain, but this emperor really liked to look in the

mirror, and he was also quite stupid. listen.


The emperor in this story loved nothing more than trying on new clothes.

In fact, all he thought about was clothes all day long. He had twelve large wardrobes, and they were all crammed full. And in his palace there were mirrors everywhere.

There were at least three in every hallway, eight in the dining room, and ten in the large drawing room, not to mention the bedroom.

There was a huge one nailed to the ceiling, so he could even look at himself in bed, so you can imagine he often lay on his back.


Everyone in the country was talking about how vain the emperor was. Two strangers who happened to be traveling through the country and had no money in their wallets heard about it. They came up with a clever plan to make a lot of money in an easy way.


They went to the palace and told the emperor that they could make clothes that were not only very beautiful, but also very special. The emperor immediately sat up. Of course he had to have those clothes.

“What do you mean, special?” he asked.

"Only smart people can see the clothes," the men explained. "To stupid people they are invisible."


So these clothes are not only beautiful and special, thought the emperor, but also very useful, because now I can find out who is stupid and who is not. And so you see that the emperor himself was not very smart. Because who would believe something like that?


The emperor ordered the two liars to begin immediately, because two weeks later there would be a large procession through the city and he wanted

walking ahead in his new clothes. He showed them a large room where they could set up their looms.

The two men asked the emperor for handfuls of money to buy the most beautiful, the finest and therefore the most expensive yarns. But they kept that money for themselves. They bought nothing at all. Every day they went to the looms. They pretended to stretch yarn, they pretended to weave, they pretended to take the most beautiful fabrics from the looms and they pretended to make the most

beautiful clothes from these fabrics. But there was no fabric, there was no clothes, there was nothing at all.


The emperor was naturally very curious about what the clothes looked like. He sent his chief minister to look. One of the men let him in and pointed to the empty looms and the empty tables.

'Just look,' he said, 'do you see how beautifully the fabrics shine? And do you see how beautiful the clothes have become there on the table?'


The prime minister looked at the empty looms and the empty tables and thought: Oh dear, I am stupid, because I don't see any clothes. But of course he didn't

say that out loud, because then everyone would know he was stupid. What he said was: 'Yes-yes-yes-yes, they are really beautiful!'


And when the emperor asked a moment later how the clothes were, the prime minister said again that they were beautiful. 'Aha!' said the emperor to himself. 'So this minister is not stupid, because he can see the clothes.'

Everyone in the country heard about the two wonderful tailors and everyone talked about the emperor's new clothes. The emperor himself became more and more

curious, but he had to wait until the day of the great procession. Only then was he finally allowed to admire his new clothes.


With all his ministers in a row behind him, he went to the room of the two impostors. Of course, the emperor didn't see the clothes either. Oh dear, he thought, so I'm

stupid. And of course he didn't want anyone to know that either.

'Well-well-well,' he said, 'they are really magnificent!' 'Yes-yes-yes,' said all the ministers, who also saw nothing. 'They are really magnificent!'

The king undressed completely to put on the new clothes.

The two men pretended to dress him. First they put on a pair of underwear, then a

shirt, trousers, a jacket and the rest. Then they took a few steps back and looked at the naked emperor from head to toe.


"Yes," they said, "the clothes really suit you!" "Yes-yes-yes," cried all the ministers, "the clothes really suit you!"


Proudly the emperor walked out and in his bare clothes he strode forward at the head of the procession. The streets were packed with people. Everyone was curious

about the emperor's new clothes and everyone shouted: 'Oh, oh, oh, what beautiful clothes!' Because no one wants to look stupid, that was clear once again.


But suddenly there was a little boy who shouted: 'But he's not wearing any clothes at all!'


The people around him looked at the boy in shock. And then they looked at each other again. And do you know what happens when one dares to tell the truth?

Then suddenly others dare to do it too. So a moment later everyone shouted: 'The emperor walks naked, the emperor walks naked!'


I'm clearly not the only one who's stupid, thought the emperor. He straightened his

back and walked even more proudly through the people. In the nude! How do you like that!

So lock the door tight when I'm gone. You're only allowed to open it when it's me, and for no one else. Did you understand that?' She was that careful! And she was right, because listen…


One day, mother goat had to go shopping again. This time, someone stood behind the big tree next to the house and watched her until she disappeared around the bend. That someone crept up to the house, walked around it and sniffed at the walls and windows. You guessed it, that was the wolf!

He knocked on the door and said in his sweetest voice, "Open up, dear goats, I am your mother." Inside, the

goats cried out in fear, "No, no, you sound very different from our mother. Our mother does not have such a deep voice!" They huddled together and trembled with fear.

That's right, thought the wolf, I must speak in a high voice. He walked back to the thick tree, waited a while, and then walked back to the door.


In a high-pitched voice he said: 'Dear little goats, open the door, it is I, your mother.'

That

sounded like their mother, the little goats thought, but to be on the safe side they called out: 'Then let me see your paw under the door.' They shrank back,

because through the crack under the door they saw a brown hairy paw! 'No, no,' they cried, trembling, their eyes wide with fear, 'you are not our mother. Our mother has white paws!' That is true, said the wolf to himself. He went to his

den and came back with a pot of flour. He put his paw through it and then he said:

in a high-pitched voice: 'Dear goats, open the door! It is I, your mother.' The goats all stood in

front of the door and shouted: 'Show me your paw!'


When they saw the white paw, they opened the door. And they shouldn't have done that...

The wolf jumped inside. The goats screamed and flew in all directions. One crawled under the table, the second crawled behind the cupboard, the third crawled under the bed, the fourth crawled into the laundry basket, the fifth crawled behind the coat rack, the sixth crawled behind a door. The seventh crawled into the grandfather clock… The wolf found them all and ate them up immediately – all except one… Only the seventh kid, which was in the clock, he had not found.


Not long after, the mother goat returned. She had seen from a distance that the door was open, and when she found the pot of flour by the door and her goats nowhere to be seen, she began to wail terribly. 'Children, children,' she cried, 'where are you?'


The seventh goat carefully emerged from the clock and told what had happened.


"One thing is certain," said mother goat, "if he has eaten six of my babies, he

will now have a very big belly and with a big belly like that you won't get far." She grabbed a pair of

scissors and a sewing box and went outside. There was flour everywhere, you could see where the wolf had gone. And sure enough, a little further on he was

sleeping under a tree.


"Look at his big belly," said mother goat to herself, "that's where my darlings are." She tiptoed closer and quickly cut his belly open. The kids jumped out. Mother goat filled the belly with big stones and sewed everything up neatly. Then they all hid behind the trees.

struggling with all those stones in his stomach, he bent over to drink, fell into the water and drowned.

Mother goat and her seven kids jumped up and down, they were so happy that the big bad wolf was dead. Relieved, they went home. They took

the pot of flour inside and baked delicious pancakes with it.

ONCE upon a time – long, long ago – there was a girl called Little Red Riding Hood.

That wasn't her real name, but everyone called her that because she always wore a red hood on her head.


One day her mother said to her: 'Little Red Riding Hood, you must go to Grandma's.

She is ill and I have a basket of rolls, cookies and a bottle of good wine for her.' Grandma lived in a small house, in the

middle of the forest near the big oak tree. It was far away, but Little Red Riding Hood knew the way very well.

"Remember," said Mother, "to walk briskly and not to stray from the path." Little Red Riding Hood promised. She walked into the forest and indeed walked briskly.


Suddenly a big wolf poked his head out from between the trees. Little Red Riding Hood greeted her politely, but kept walking briskly. 'Little girl, little girl,' said the wolf, 'what a hurry! Where are you going with your basket?' 'To grandma,' answered

Little Red Riding Hood. 'She is ill.'


“Oh? And where does Grandma live?” asked the wolf again.

'By the big oak,' said Little Red Riding Hood, 'but please excuse me, I have no time now, I must go on. Good-bye, Mr. Wolf!'


The wolf walked along with her and said, 'Well, well, don't you see what a lovely day it is today? And look at all those beautiful flowers. Your grandmother will be very happy when you give her the basket of goodies and a beautiful bunch of flowers.'


Little Red Riding Hood didn't know that the wolf was very hungry and that Little Red Riding Hood seemed like a tasty morsel to him. But the wolf was greedy. Now that he had

heard about that grandmother, he actually wanted Little Red Riding Hood and Grandma as a tasty morsel.

"I mustn't leave the path," said Little Red Riding Hood.

"Come, come!" said the wolf. "Isn't it for your grandmother?" And when he had said that, he disappeared among the trees.

Little Red Riding Hood stood still. The wolf is right, she thought, those flowers are beautiful. She forgot her mother's advice, and… went off the path.


Meanwhile the wolf ran with great leaps to the big oak. He stopped at the little house. 'Grandma,' he called, 'I am Little Red Riding Hood, will you open the door for me?' 'I can't,

dear,' called Grandma. 'I am in bed. Just pull the string and the door will open by itself.'


The wolf pulled the string, pushed open the door, jumped on Grandma's bed and swallowed her whole in one gulp. Then he got a nightgown out of the closet, put Grandma's nightcap on his head, put her glasses on his nose and got under the covers. It looked like he was Grandma now.

Meanwhile, Little Red Riding Hood was on her way again, now with a large bunch of flowers.

At grandma's house she looked up strangely: the door was open. So she walked straight inside, to the bedroom.


She looked at her grandmother in surprise, because she looked a bit strange.


“But Grandma, what big ears you have!” she cried in surprise.

The wolf said in a high-pitched voice, "That's so I can hear you better, dear." "But, Grandma,"

said Little Red Riding Hood again, "what a big nose you have!" "That's so I can smell you better, child," said the wolf.

"But Grandma, what big eyes you have!" said Little Red Riding Hood now.

The wolf replied, "That's so I can see you better, dear."


"But Grandma, what a big mouth you have!" Little Red Riding Hood was amazed.

“That's the better to eat you,” cried the wolf, who had become extremely hungry by now.

He jumped up and ate Little Red Riding Hood in one bite, just like he had done with Grandma. Growling with satisfaction, he went back to bed.

He fell asleep right away (Yes, that's what happens when you eat that much!) and started snoring really loudly.


Now, Grandma had a good friend who was a hunter. That hunter happened to be walking past the cottage that afternoon and heard the wolf snoring.

'That's not Granny snoring like that,' said the hunter to himself. 'Something's wrong here.

And the door's open, that's wrong too.' He went inside, saw a

basket of goodies with a bunch of flowers next to it, saw a pair of broken glasses lying on the floor, and then he saw the wolf in the bed.


He understood at once that the wolf had eaten Grandma. And that basket, I know it, thought the hunter. That is Little Red Riding Hood's.

Without thinking further, he grabbed his hunting knife and cut open the wolf's belly. Grandma and Little Red Riding Hood quickly stepped out. 'Phew, pff, it was so stuffy in there,' they cried in relief.

"Shhh!" hissed the hunter. "Shhh, we mustn't wake the wolf."


From outside he brought three large stones, stuffed them into the wolf's belly and sewed the belly shut again. Then all three of them hid in the big wardrobe. Through a crack they

kept an eye on the wolf.


After a while the wolf woke up. 'Oh,' he groaned, rubbing his belly, 'I feel a little sick.' (No wonder with those big rocks in his belly, but of course he didn't know that.) 'And how thirsty I am!' The wolf dragged himself outside to the stream. He bent down to drink, but because of the heavy rocks in his belly he fell forward into the water and drowned.


“Hurray!” shouted Little Red Riding Hood, the huntsman, and Grandma, who had seen it all.

Grandma quickly went back under the covers, because she was still not feeling

well. The huntsman and Little Red Riding Hood both took a chair and the three of them ate the rolls and cookies with relish and drank the wine of Little Red Riding Hood's mother.


"Mmmmmm!" said Grandma. "Your mother sure can bake delicious cookies, Little Red Riding Hood!"

The hunter and Little Red Riding Hood also wanted to say something, but their mouths were full. And you're not supposed to talk with your mouth full. Everyone knows that.

and no. Yes, because he was quite proud of all those beautiful girls. And no, because they gave him a headache. He was puzzled. For weeks he had to buy new shoes for his eleven daughters every day. Eleven pairs of shoes every day, that's seventy-seven pairs of shoes in one week. And why? Because the shoes he had bought the day before were already worn out the next day: the soles were hanging loose,

the heels were worn crooked, the straps torn, and so on.


How could that happen? How did those shoes become completely worn out and broken in one night? And it was even weirder when you knew that the king locked his daughters in their bedroom every night at exactly eight o'clock. They couldn't go anywhere!


When he asked, the eleven daughters lowered their eyes and remained silent in all languages. The king was completely mad about it. And all those shoes cost him a lot of money. In desperation, the king placed an advertisement in the newspaper.


Whoever solved the riddle of the worn-out shoes was allowed to marry one of the princesses. You were allowed to spend three nights in the girls' bedroom, but if you still hadn't solved the riddle, your head was chopped off.


Weeks passed. Many men had tried to solve the riddle, but they had all fallen asleep at some point and so they could not say how all those shoes had become worn out.


A soldier, who was no longer so young and had been wounded in the war, wanted to try it too. But first he went to an old wise woman for advice.


The woman gave him a cloak that made you invisible when you put it on. And she also gave him some wise advice: 'Be especially careful that you

'Don't drink

anything!' The soldier didn't quite understand this, but he decided to follow her advice anyway.


The next day he knocked on the palace gate. The king let him in and said that he hoped that the riddle would now finally be solved.

When the clock struck eight, the princesses went to bed. The soldier got the key to the bedroom and locked himself in with the princesses.

He put the key in his pocket.


Meanwhile, the girls were getting ready for the night. The youngest gave the soldier a glass of red wine. 'You were wounded, weren't you?' she said kindly. 'You really need to get some strength. A good glass of wine will do you good!'

No one had ever seen him laugh, and then of course there was that strange blue beard…

The wedding was celebrated, but before the girl left for the castle with the seventy rooms, she whispered quickly to her three brothers: 'If you hear me cry for help, come to me at once!' The brothers promised, and with a knot in her stomach the girl

got into the carriage. It was three days' drive through dense forests and misty marshes, and then the girl sat there, alone with Bluebeard in the great, gloomy castle,

A month later, Bluebeard had to travel unexpectedly. He gave her a large bunch of keys. 'Here are the keys to all the rooms,' he said. 'You may go anywhere except one room. This small key is to a room in the

The girl was curious and immediately tried out all the keys. She didn’t know that there were so many types of rooms – one had red wallpaper, another green, the third had a marble floor and the fourth had expensive carpets on the wall. Every day she saw

It wasn't a big door, no, it was a very ordinary door. She felt the rough wood and the big black hinges. How strange, really, that she wasn't allowed in there.


That night she couldn't sleep. She kept thinking about that room. What was in there? Why wasn't she allowed to know?

Fortunately, her sister Anna came to stay, so she had something else to

Tomorrow Bluebeard would come home and what if he asked for the little key…
